Project Description
OVERVIEW
  • Motivation
    • A key component of professional growth at universities might be regarded to be inter-professional abilities, notably teamwork in this study. It operates at the human level via cultural and knowledge aspects when it reaches the community level, which means that professional viewpoint may cause conflict and moral ambiguity about professional duties in project management [1]. In addition, challenges might arise from unease, ambiguous job assignment, diverse work cultures, and conflicting versions of information [2]. Therefore, it is advised that students actively seek out teammates that meet their expectations when it comes to collaboration. This will ensure that the relationships between team members are positive, resulting in their willingness to assist, accept, and like one another [3].
    Group assignments were prioritised in the majority of the courses offered ever since RMIT acknowledged the value of teamwork. In face-to-face classrooms, on Canvas, or via social media, RMIT students may discover teammates both online and offline. However, as there isn't yet a platform designed just for RMIT students, their messages may not reach the correct people, and it will take a lot of effort for them to connect and share information to make sure their potential teammates fit their expectations. After identifying the insights, our group created TinMate, a website that helps RMIT students discover teammates.
  • Topic and Landscape
    • Since gen Z is using dating apps more and more, especially Tinder [4], which is the most popular app in the field, we decided to make the UI/UX of the TinMate website similar to Tinder to make it more user-friendly and familiar. The site will work like other dating apps, but in this prototype, the filter and its criteria will be changed to match the site's goal, which is to find teammates. For example, the GPA, the goal and strengths of the team, etc., will be used as filters. Also, familiar features of dating apps like viewing profiles, UI chat boxes, matching, SuperLike, and reporting will stay the same. Also, since this site is only for RMIT students, you will need to show a screenshot of your student ID card when you sign in.
    After that, we realised that, even though there are a lot of international and exchange students at RMIT, most of the posts about finding teammates on social media are in Vietnamese. This makes it hard for international students to understand and respond to the posts, and it's also a shame for local students because they might miss the chance to meet the right teammates. So, (name of website) will only use English to make sure everything works together.
    It is hoped that the TinMate website will not only fix the problems with the old ways, but also give tech-savvy RMIT students a new, fun, and easy way to find teammates by using the same UI/UX as dating apps. Taking everything into account, our team thinks that this site can help RMIT students save time and effort while still being able to work with the people they were supposed to work with.

    • Even though there are many other methods for the software development life cycle, our team decided to use Scrum as our main way to work together. This is because we need an agile framework that is quick, flexible, and efficient. As a result, Scrum makes it easier to build and deliver products during our development phase

      Project background story
    During our Initiation Phase, we had a team meeting to think about what kind of project that we should develop. We were aiming for something innovative and unique. Additionally, the project should also have the objective of helping RMIT students. It took us a while for us to finally come up with something like a Tinder-clone but with an educational intention, which is searching for teammates. If you don’t know about Tinder, basically it’s a dating social network where you can see the details (or biography) of other people such as their interests, occupations, ages, etc. As we all had used Tinder before, we had no trouble imagining how our project would work.
    Moving on to the Planning and Estimation Phase, we had another meeting to decide what tools that we should use and tasks allocation for each member. We agreed that we would use GitHub as our version control system and code hosting platform. About the Technologies, at first, we decided to only make the user interface for our tinder-clone website with the use of HTML and CSS. However, upon some discussions, we then decided to use the basic ReactJS and take advantage of the ‘tinder-card-swipe’ module in the NPM package with CSS and in-line CSS.
    The final obstacle that we face is with the deployment of the website, as we also aim for the interactive and responsive design, we choose Netlify for our deployment platform. However, it was very difficult and prone-to-error due to the number of bugs that we encountered and our lack of knowledge for Javascript. At the end, thanks to our persistence, we overcame all difficulties and finalised our web app. With that being said, as we often finished our allocated tasks early, we decided to go overboard and turn our website into a web app.

Risks
  • A. Internal Risk
    1. Inadequate Expertise
      2. Because we don't know much about web design, the final product might not work as well or meet quality standards as the prototype. This could mean that planned scope features can't be finished because there isn't enough time or understanding to prepare. To deal with this, our team will meet twice a week to make sure that the projects and members are working steadily and getting good insurance. The team leader also checks on the member's status and tries to think of all the things that could happen to other members, like missing meetings or quitting. Each member of the team should know what their specific tasks and responsibilities are, and it is recommended that they spend the first few weeks learning the basics of HTML and CSS to help them understand the project and do a better job.
    2. Scope Creep:
      3. The project's scope could change, and the work might be more than what the team can handle or what was initially planned. Scope creep is most often caused by unauthorised changes or miscommunications between members, which can lead to poor performance and design systems. Our team decided to avoid this by making a clear project timeline, scope, and out-of-scope to make sure what the project would include and be able to archive when it was done. We also keep track of the projects' schedules and update the project's scope statement when things change, making sure to find and document any important changes.
    3. Technical risk: Version control
      4. When we all work together to build the website, it's possible that someone will accidentally delete or mess up some lines of code, which would cause the website to crash without us knowing. If someone wants to add a new feature to the website, we will use the git checkout branch command to fix the problem. After that, they can ask the team to look over their work by sending a "pull request." This can make sure the origin master branch is safe.
  • B. External Risk
    1. Human error
      2. Even though Tinder's user interface and user experience (UI/UX) is used, TinMate only has one goal: to help RMIT students build their professional networks and connect with peers who meet their expectations. There is a chance, though, that some people will use this app for things other than finding teammates or building professional networks. Ellison and Hancock [5] said that one of the main reasons is that traditional signals, like intelligence, skills, characteristics, and so on, are easy to fake online. In the real world, on the other hand, it will be much easier to find out if someone is lying because they can talk to them or watch them. That goes against what TinMate is trying to do because it will take other users longer and more work to find other ones. To deal with the problem of human error, we will tell other users to report any account that seems to be going against TinMate's goal
    2. Server overload or attack
      3. Since the project will be available to all RMIT students, server bottlenecks will happen often at times like the beginning of a semester or course, when students are using the app to find their teammates. When the app gets popular, someone will try to steal our clients' data or DDOS our server, which could lead to a bad user experience, lost customers, and lower profits. We would set up a more powerful server that can handle a large number of users. We will also make sure our website is always up-to-date and back up our data.
    3. Technical risk: Website future performance
      4. We are currently using the out-of-date Material UI v4 open-source React component package, which could cause the website to crash in the future. In other words, if we don't do any more maintenance on our website, the API might not be stable enough to handle Material UI v4, which is now in v5, which could cause the website to load very slowly or even crash. So, even after the project is done, we will keep it up-to-date and take care of it on a regular basis.
